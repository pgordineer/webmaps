ABSTRACT

Water demands for producing hydrocarbons from the Marcellus Shale via hydraulic fracturing present significant infrastructure and environmental challenges. Traditional water sourcing and storage methods, primarily the process of trucking water across public and private roadways, im- pose heavy burdens on public infrastructure. This heavy trucking not only exacerbates traffic congestion, causes visual and auditory disturbances for local communities, and damages road net- works servicing these gas operations, but in doing so also imposes financial strains on often under- funded public entities and increases net carbon emissions linked to energy production. Freshwater impoundments represent a sustainable water management solution, enabling large-scale pumped water storage and captured rainwater for hydraulic fracturing. This study employs a geographic information system (GIS)-based approach to investigate the capacity of existing freshwater im- poundments on public lands within the Marcellus Shale region. It assesses their potential to meet the ongoing water demands of continued hydraulic fracturing by analyzing active impoundment footprints, storage capacities, and annual precipitation capture rates. This research further ex- amines impoundment withdrawals for wells completed in the Marcellus Shale, calculating water demand per well of a given production length and the water use per unit of gas delivered to mar- ket. The findings emphasize that the carbon footprint is not the sole environmental impact of shale gas usage; water management also plays a critical role. By demonstrating the potential of fresh- water impoundments to support efficient and sustainable water practices and reduce infrastructure burdens, this study contributes valuable insights for strategic infrastructure planning. It aims to mitigate environmental impacts and enhance water security for hydraulic fracturing operations and the surrounding communities and ecosystems.


Keywords: infrastructure planning, marcellus shale, water management, spatial analysis, public lands, freshwater impoundments, sustainability

INTRODUCTION

The Marcellus Shale, a vast underground rock formation spanning parts of Pennsylvania, West Virginia, Ohio, and other nearby states, holds significant natural gas reserves. The extraction of this hydrocarbon resource through the process of hydraulic fracturing (“fracking”) has revolution- ized the energy landscape, contributing to significant growth in regional energy production and economic growth. However, this boom in hydrocarbon production comes alongside significant environmental challenges, particularly concerning water usage and its impact on surrounding in- frastructure and ecosystems.

Hydraulic fracturing requires substantial amounts of water, typically millions of gallons per well, for the process of fracturing the shale rock and releasing the trapped natural gas (Duthu and

Bradley (1), Jiang et al. (2), Risser (3), Soeder and Kappel (4)). Traditionally, this water is sourced from surface waters, such as rivers and streams, and transported to well sites via truck, leading to heavy burdens on public infrastructure. This trucking of water not only contributes to traffic congestion, road damage, and increased maintenance costs, but also generates air pollution and greenhouse gas emissions, impacting both local communities and the global environment (Kille (5), Tsapakis (6)). The search for sustainable water management solutions has increased interest in freshwater impoundments. These impoundments, constructed on or near well pads, can store large volumes of pumped water and captured rainwater, providing a readily available water source for hydraulic fracturing operations (Duthu and Bradley (1)). By utilizing impoundments, the in- dustry can reduce its reliance on traditional water transport methods and minimize the negative impacts of water trucking, including road damage, traffic congestion, air pollution, and greenhouse gas emissions. This study investigates the potential of freshwater impoundments on public lands within the Marcellus Shale region to meet the water demands of hydraulic fracturing operations. Employing a Geographic Information System (GIS)-based approach, this study analyzes active im- poundments’ existing footprint, storage capacities, and annual precipitation capture rates to assess their potential contribution to sustainable water management. Furthermore, this research examines impoundment withdrawals for hydraulically fractured wells, calculating the water demand per well based on factors related to its planned development, as well as calculating the water use per unit of gas delivered to market. This research aims to demonstrate that the environmental factors of shale gas extract extend beyond the carbon emissions directly associated with its use; water management plays a critical role. By highlighting the potential of freshwater impoundments to support efficient and sustainable water practices, this study contributes valuable insights for strategic infrastructure planning and decision-making. Ultimately, this study aims to promote solutions that mitigate the environmental impacts of hydraulic fracturing and enhance water security for the industry and the surrounding communities and ecosystems.

LITERATURE REVIEW

The water demands of Marcellus Shale gas extraction via hydraulic fracturing pose significant challenges to infrastructure and environmental sustainability. This research focuses on the potential of freshwater impoundments as sustainable solutions for water management, aiming to alleviate the burdens associated with traditional water trucking methods. This literature review explores existing research covering water usage, wastewater management, infrastructure impacts, and the role of freshwater impoundments.

Water usage and Its Impacts

Hydraulic fracturing in the Marcellus Shale requires large volumes of water, typically millions of gallons per well (Duthu and Bradley (1), Jiang et al. (2), Risser (3), Soeder and Kappel (4)). This high water demand has raised concerns about the sustainability of water withdrawals, par- ticularly in areas with limited water resources or competing demands. Research has shown that surface water sources, such as rivers and streams, are predominantly used for fracking, raising potential ecological concerns related to dewatering and impacts on aquatic life (Hansen et al. (7), Pen (8)). While the use of recycled flowback water has been increasing (Hansen et al. (7), Duthu and Bradley (1), Jiang et al. (2)), it still represents a relatively small portion of total water usage (Hansen et al. (7)). Consequently, exploring alternative water sourcing and storage methods, in- cluding freshwater impoundments, becomes crucial for minimizing the environmental footprint of

hydraulic fracturing.


Wastewater Management

Fracking generates substantial amounts of wastewater, including flowback water, produced water, and other waste streams (Hansen et al. (7)). The management of this wastewater poses environmen- tal, infrastructure, and logistical challenges and requires careful consideration of disposal options.

Reuse and Recycling

Reuse and recycling: Recycling and reusing flowback water for subsequent fracking operations reduces freshwater demand and minimizes waste disposal needs (Duthu and Bradley (1), Hansen et al. (7)). However, treatment and dilution with freshwater are often required before reuse, limiting its impact on reducing freshwater withdrawals.

Underground Injection Control Wells (UIC)

Disposal of produced water in deep underground wells is a common practice but raises concerns about potential groundwater contamination, induced seismicity, and the permanent removal of water from the hydrologic cycle (Hansen et al. (7)).

Treatment and Discharge to Surface Waters

Treatment of wastewater for discharge into surface waters presents challenges related to the effi- cacy of removing contaminants and the potential for impacting water quality downstream (Hansen et al. (7), Olmstead et al. (9)).

Infrastructure Impacts of Water Trucking

The traditional method of trucking water to and from well sites significantly burdens public in- frastructure. The heavy truck traffic associated with water hauling contributes to road damage, traffic congestion, and increased maintenance costs for road networks (Kille2014, Tsapakis2020). Studies have shown a correlation between increased truck traffic and higher crash rates, posing safety risks for local communities (Brasier and Perchinski (10), Graham et al. (11)). The financial burden of repairing and maintaining roads falls on public entities, often straining limited budgets. Additionally, the extensive use of trucks for water transport contributes to increased carbon emis- sions and air pollution (Korfmacher et al. (12)), a factor in both local air quality and global climate change.

Freshwater Impoundments as a Sustainable Solution

Freshwater impoundments offer a promising solution for sustainable water management in the con- text of hydraulic fracturing. They provide large-scale storage capacity for pumped water and cap- tured rainwater, reducing reliance on traditional water sources and mitigating the negative impacts of water trucking (Duthu and Bradley (1)). The industry can minimize truck traffic by utilizing impoundments, also reducing road damage, traffic congestion, and greenhouse gas emissions. Ad- ditionally, impoundments can enhance water security by providing a readily available water supply for fracking operations, decreasing vulnerability to droughts or water restrictions.

GIS and Spatial Analysis for Infrastructure Planning

Geographic Information Systems are rapidly growing in use as a powerful tool for infrastructure planning and environmental management (Morovatdar et al. (13)). GIS enables spatial analysis of

impoundment locations, sizes, and proximity to well sites, facilitating the optimization of water sourcing and distribution networks. Additionally, GIS can be used to assess the environmental impacts of impoundment construction and identify suitable locations for future impoundments, ensuring a balance between water resource development and ecological protection.

METHODOLOGY

This study employs a Geographic Information System (GIS)-based approach to assess the capacity of existing freshwater impoundments on public lands within the Marcellus Shale region and to evaluate their potential to meet the water demands of hydraulic fracturing operations.

Shale Gas Infrastructure and Land Ownership

Using Pennsylvania’s Spatial Data Access (PAS (14)), a data portal maintained by the Pennsyl- vania State University, spatial data can be extracted for usage in ArcGIS Online and ArcGIS Pro to analyze numerous datasets. This study will focus on the feature classes for the locations and boundaries of existing freshwater impoundments on public lands, publicly owned State Forest lands, and State Forest leased lands. This dataset also includes feature classes for other infrastruc- ture, including natural gas well pads, compression stations, pipelines, and oil and gas well top-hole coordinate locations. The data for freshwater impoundments includes information on the operating company, approval date, construction date, and classification. However, the data notably excludes information on total water capacity. Due to the absence of an available dataset for impoundment capacity, the volume for each must be calculated.

Precipitation Rates

Historic annual precipitation data was sourced via BIL (Band Interleaved by Line) raster from the PRISM (Parameter-elevation Regressions on Independent Slopes Model) dataset maintained by

Oregon State University and the Northwest Alliance for Computational Science and Engineering (PRI (15)).

Water Usage and Gas Production Data

Statistics for Marcellus shale gas well production, average lengths, and total drainage area were compiled from the National Energy Technology Laboratory (Boswell (16))

Data Processing and Analysis

Regulatory Driven Assumptions

Based on Pennsylvania Regulations (PA8 (17), 25P (18)), the maximum depth of 15 feet and a 3:1 slope for impoundment berms can be assumed.

GIS Processing

The buffering tool is utilized to create internal polygons within each impoundment boundary rep- resenting the area of maximum depth (15 feet). Berm polygons are created using the erase tool to subtract the internal polygons from each impoundment boundary, thus representing the 3:1 sloped berm area. The square-foot area is then calculated using the geometry calculation tool for both the sloped berm border and the internal polygons. These values are then joined to the table of the total impoundment utilizing the spatial join tool.

Using the joined values, the field calculator can calculate the volume by summing the volume of the maximum depth area (area x 15 feet depth) and half the volume of the berm area (area x 15 feet / 2) to account for the sloping section of the berm. This results in a total volume in cubic feet, which can then be multiplied by 7.48 in the field calculator to obtain the resulting impoundment capacity in gallons.

Impoundment Capacity in Truck Equivalents

Divide the calculated volume of each impoundment (in gallons) by 5,000 (assuming a standard truck capacity of 5,000 gallons) to determine the equivalent number of truckloads needed to fill the impoundment.

Infrastructure cost per impoundment

Utilizing the average infrastructure damage cost per mile per truck from Wu et al. (Wu et al. (19)), which is $3.24 for a loaded 5,000-gallon water truck. Assume an average trip distance of 20 miles for water trucks. The infrastructure cost per truck trip can be calculated as (20 miles x $3.24/mile). The cost per gallon transported can be calculated as ($64.8 per trip / 5,000 gallons). This value is used to calculate the estimated infrastructure damage cost reduction per impoundment capacity. The field calculator is used to multiply the cost per truck trip by the number of trucks required to fill each impoundment capacity.

Impoundment Precipitation Capture calculation

The zonal statistics tool in ArcGIS Pro is used to calculate the average annual precipitation in millimeters for each impoundment based on the PRISM rainfall raster data (PRI (15)). The average precipitation data is converted in the field calculator to units of feet. The average precipitation (in feet) is multiplied by the total area of each impoundment (in square feet), and the result is converted to gallons (multiplied by 7.48) to estimate the annual precipitation capture potential of each impoundment (in gallons). This capture potential can then be divided by 5,000 to determine the number of truck trips offset directly by precipitation capture. The capture potential of the impoundment can be multiplied by the cost per gallon transported, as previously calculated, in order to calculate the infrastructure damage costs offset directly by precipitation capture.

Project Area Water Demand Analysis

Water usage per acre

Utilizing the average Marcellus wellbore drainage area of 111 acres (Boswell (16)) and the average water usage of 4 million gallons (Jiang et al. (2), Risser (3), Pen (8)), the water usage per acre of drainage area is calculated (4 million gallons / 111 acres).

Water use per unit of energy

Utilizing the average Estimated Ultimate Recovery (EUR) per well of 4.02Bcf and average water usage of 4 million gallons (Boswell (16)), the average water usage per Mcf can be calculated (4 million gallons/4.02Bcf).

DISCUSSION

The results of this study highlight the significant potential of freshwater impoundments on public lands within the Marcellus Shale region as a sustainable solution for managing water demands of hydraulic fracturing operations. By analyzing the storage capacity of these impoundments and their potential for capturing rainwater, we can assess their ability to reduce reliance on traditional water sourcing methods and mitigate the associated infrastructure and environmental impacts.

Impoundment Capacity and Water Demand

The combined storage capacity of the 30 analyzed freshwater impoundments demonstrates a sub- stantial potential for water storage and supply. When considered alongside the estimated water

usage per well and acre of wellbore drainage area and the infrastructure costs associated with al- ternatives, this capacity suggests that strategically placed and well-managed impoundments could significantly contribute to meeting the water needs of hydraulic fracturing operations while reduc- ing overall impact.

Further investigation into freshwater impoundments would provide a more precise under- standing of their ability to meet the dynamic water demands of the industry consistently. Future works could focus on developing suitability models for strategic and precise placement of fresh- water impoundments. Additional work could be completed to compile current water transport pipelines to determine the service area of existing or potential freshwater impoundments. Further studies may explore the long-term sustainability of these impoundments, as well as evaporation rates, sedimentation, and liner leakage rates. Future efforts should also focus towards public en- gagement; prior works have studied public response to trucking traffic, but not public opinion of freshwater impoundment infrastructure on public lands.

CONCLUSIONS

This study has shed light on the significant potential of freshwater impoundments as a sustainable water management solution for hydraulic fracturing operations within the Marcellus Shale region. By analyzing the storage capacity of existing impoundments on public lands and their potential for capturing rainwater, we have demonstrated a viable alternative to traditional water sourcing methods that heavily rely on trucking. The findings of this study highlight several key conclusions.

Impoundments offer substantial storage capacity

The combined storage capacity of the 22 analyzed impoundments demonstrates their potential to significantly contribute to meeting the water demands of hydraulic fracturing, reducing reliance on alternative water management methods, and minimizing the associated environmental impacts.

-Rainfall capture presents a sustainable water source: The analysis of annual rainfall capture poten- tial emphasizes the ability of impoundments to harness precipitation as a sustainable water source, reducing reliance on water withdrawals from surface and groundwater sources and minimizing associated ecological disruptions.

Infrastructure cost reduction is significant

Utilizing impoundments instead of trucking water leads to substantial reductions in truck traffic, resulting in considerable cost savings associated with road maintenance and repairs. This alleviates the financial burden on public entities responsible for infrastructure upkeep and contributes to a more economically sustainable approach to shale gas development.

Environmental benefits are multifaceted

By minimizing truck traffic, impoundments contribute to a smaller environmental footprint by reducing greenhouse gas emissions, air pollution, and noise pollution. Additionally, capturing and utilizing accumulated precipitation through impoundments promotes water conservation and protects sensitive aquatic ecosystems.

This study underscores the importance of strategic planning and GIS analysis in optimizing the placement and utilization of freshwater impoundments. By considering factors such as prox- imity to well sites, accessibility, and environmental impacts, we can ensure that impoundments are developed and managed to maximize their benefits while minimizing potential risks.

While the carbon footprint of natural gas usage is often the focus of environmental concerns related to shale gas development, this study emphasizes the critical role of water management. Freshwater impoundments offer a practical and effective solution for reducing the environmental impact of hydraulic fracturing and enhancing water security for the industry and the surrounding communities.

Moving forward, continued research and collaboration among stakeholders are crucial steps towards promoting the adoption of sustainable water management practices and ensuring respon- sible continued development of the Marcellus Shale resource. By embracing solutions such as freshwater impoundments, we can strive towards a future where the benefits of shale gas are bal- anced with the protection of our environment and water resources for generations to come.
